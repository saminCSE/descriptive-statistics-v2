Potential Risk of Malposition of Nasogastric Tube Using Nose-Ear-Xiphoid Measurement

Abstract
Background: The nose-ear-xiphoid (NEX) measurement is commonly used to determine the insertion depth of nasogastric tubes. However, the accuracy of this method in preventing malposition remains controversial. This study investigates the relationship between patient anatomical measurements and the risk of nasogastric tube malposition.
Methods: A retrospective analysis was conducted on patients who underwent nasogastric tube placement. Various anatomical measurements including nose-to-gastric distance (NG), ear-to-xiphoid distance (EX), and nose-ear-xiphoid distance (NEX) were recorded. Patients were categorized based on whether side-hole passage occurred during insertion.

Significant differences were observed between groups in NG length (p=0.003), EX length (p=0.020), and NEX length (p=0.036). The "No side-hole pass" group demonstrated longer NG measurements (488.6±23.4 mm) compared to the side-hole pass group (458.4±23.9 mm), while showing shorter EX (348.1±22.8 mm vs. 368.9±24.5 mm) and NEX measurements (500.0±27.0 mm vs. 523.0±28.9 mm).

Discussion
Our findings suggest that the traditional NEX measurement method may not adequately predict proper nasogastric tube placement in all patients. The significant variations in anatomical measurements between groups indicate that individual patient characteristics should be considered when determining optimal insertion depth.
